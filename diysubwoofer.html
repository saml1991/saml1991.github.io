DIY Subwoofer May 2011

Its been a long time coming but this project is nearly at a close. Through all the deliberations and many possible ways I could have gone about completing this project I got through it mainly with improvisation and what was available to me. The outcome I wanted from this was to have a subwoofer which was very loud but also worked well with both movies and music as well as be able to fit into my small bedroom. Also being the miser I am, I wanted to do it on the smallest budget possible. So without further ado here are some pictures throughout the various stages of the build.

How it came

Input sensitivity

This amp can be used for both HiFi and PA systems, the line level of a PA system is designed to travel over long distances therefore the input sensitivity is around 775mV, whereas HiFi and home audio the cables only have to travel a short distance and is therefore lower at 500mV. On the MF1000 Amp module there is a resistor that can be changed to change the sensitivity between 750 and 500mV. The resistor which came with the amp module was 390ohms meaning the sensitivity was 750mV, I could have just left it with this setup and just increased the gain through the AV receiver being sent to the sub but I decided to change it. Instead of taking the whole board off its mounting just to change one resistor I simply tacked a second resistor in parallel with the other which is pictured below. I added a 470ohm resistor to it making the total resistance around 213ohms. It still wasn't easy though as I had to fit a soldering iron, solder, and a resistor in a tiny hole without melting any of the surrounding capacitors or touching the heatsinks.

Box Design I wanted to use this sub 50/50 HT/Music so I wanted it to be able to hit the lower frequencies used in films just as well as it would the higher frequencies. I decided to build the box at a tune of ~20hz with a volume of 2.5 cu ft.

Adding the speaker terminal and putting the Amp in a box I thought it would be safer to have the amp in an enclosure rather than exposed circuitry considering the power it is capable of.

The plan was to use to just use piece of coaxial between the plug and the input, but the outer shielding wouldn't take the solder so I just used the copper core from 2 coaxial cables instead

I improvised using some cable tie and cut up bits of coaxial outer layer to stop it from pulling out

Because the sub amp had a very loud fan I wanted it out of the room for while I'm watching movies, so in order to achieve this I used a 10m coaxial cable, a knife, some solder and old phono connectors.
